⛏️ Mine Gases Reference Guide

Comprehensive gas hazard information for rescue operations

Understanding mine gases is critical for rescue operations. Mine gases can be toxic, explosive, or cause oxygen deficiency. Each gas has unique properties including molecular weight, density, toxicity levels, and behavior patterns underground.

The following guide organizes common mine gases by molecular weight (lightest to heaviest). Tap each gas tab to view detailed information including sources, hazards, health effects, and detection methods.

Key Concept: Gas behavior depends on molecular weight. Lighter gases rise toward the roof; heavier gases sink to floors and low areas. This understanding is essential for safe navigation during rescue operations.

Sources in Mines:

0.6
Characteristics
Colorless, Odorless
Primary Hazard
Explosion

Sources in Mines:
  • Decomposition of coal and organic matter
  • Natural gas seams in coal deposits
  • Most common fire damp hazard in coal mining
Hazards:
  • Explosive range: 5-15% in air (most volatile at ~10%)
  • Non-toxic but causes asphyxiation at high concentrations
  • Rises toward roof due to low density
  • Auto-ignition temperature: 537°C
  • High concentrations reduce oxygen below safe levels (<19.5%)
Health Effects:
  • Non-toxic at normal concentrations
  • High concentrations cause oxygen displacement leading to asphyxiation
  • Symptoms: headache, nausea, dizziness, loss of consciousness
  • Death occurs when oxygen < 6%
Detection & Monitoring:
  • Measured in %LEL (%Lower Explosive Limit)
  • Catalytic bead sensors
  • Continuous monitoring at roof level
  • Portable methane indicators required for rescue teams

Carbon Monoxide (CO)

Most Dangerous
Molecular Weight
28.0 g/mol
Density vs Air
0.97
IDLH
1200 ppm
Primary Hazard
Chemical Asphyxiation
Why CO is Most Dangerous:
  • Colorless, odorless, tasteless—cannot be detected by senses
  • Binds to hemoglobin 200-250 times stronger than oxygen
  • Prevents oxygen delivery to tissues (chemical asphyxiation)
  • Non-irritant—provides no warning symptoms
  • Slightly lighter than air—accumulates at roof level in closed spaces
  • Known as the "Silent Killer"
Sources in Mines:
  • Mine fires and explosions (primary source in rescue situations)
  • Blasting operations
  • Internal combustion engines
  • Slow oxidation of coal and wood
Health Effects by Concentration:
  • 25-35 ppm: Alarm level; no immediate effects
  • 100 ppm: Maximum 8-hour TWA exposure
  • 500 ppm: Can be fatal in 3 hours
  • 3,200 ppm: Headache, dizziness, possible death in 30 minutes
  • 6,400 ppm: Loss of consciousness, death in 10-15 minutes
  • 12,800 ppm: Immediate effects, death in 1-3 minutes
Detection & Monitoring:
  • Measured in ppm (parts per million)
  • Portable CO detectors mandatory for rescue teams
  • Stationary sensors at strategic points in mine airways
  • Continuous monitoring beyond fresh air base
  • SCBA/breathing apparatus required when CO detected above alarm level

19.5%

Sources of Oxygen Deficiency:
  • Respiration (oxygen consumed by workers, animals)
  • Combustion (fires, blasting)
  • Oxidation reactions (rusting, decomposition)
  • Displacement by other gases (CO₂, methane, nitrogen)
  • Ventilation system failure
Health Effects by Oxygen Level:
  • 19.5-23.5%: Normal breathing, safe
  • 16-19.5%: Impaired judgment, difficulty in coordination
  • 12-16%: Rapid breathing, fatigue, loss of coordination
  • 6-12%: Dizziness, nausea, unconsciousness possible
  • < 6%: Rapid loss of consciousness, death
  • > 23.5%: Increase fire/explosion risk
Detection & Monitoring:
  • Electrochemical sensors in all rescue equipment
  • Initial and continuous monitoring required
  • SCBA mandatory if O₂ < 19.5%

Hydrogen Sulfide (H₂S)

Stink Damp
Molecular Weight
34.0 g/mol
Density vs Air
1.19
IDLH
100 ppm
Odor
Rotten egg
Sources in Mines:
  • Decomposition of organic materials containing sulfur
  • Strata gases from mineral deposits
  • Reactions of sulfide minerals with water
  • Less common than CO or methane but highly toxic when present
Hazards:
  • Chemical asphyxiant (inhibits cellular respiration)
  • Sinks to low areas (denser than air)
  • Flammable and explosive in range 4-44%
  • Odor can anesthetize nose at low concentrations (creates false sense of safety)
  • Very toxic at low concentrations
Health Effects by Concentration:
  • 0.13 ppm: Threshold of odor
  • 1 ppm: Slight symptoms, eye irritation
  • 10-50 ppm: Headache, dizziness, nausea, coughing
  • 50-100 ppm: Cough, severe eye irritation, loss of smell after 2-5 min
  • 100-200 ppm: Very dangerous, severe respiratory irritation, shock, convulsions, coma
  • 200-700 ppm: Severe eye/respiratory irritation, possible death within 1 hour
  • 1000-2000 ppm: Death within minutes
  • > 2000 ppm: Immediate death
Detection & Monitoring:
  • Electrochemical H₂S detectors
  • Distinctive rotten egg smell (not reliable due to olfactory adaptation)
  • Maximum safe level: 0.005% (5 ppm)
  • SCBA required when H₂S detected above safe limits

Sources in Mines:
  • Blasting operations (oxidation of nitrogen compounds)
  • Diesel engine exhaust
  • Internal combustion equipment
  • Formation after explosions and fires
Hazards:
  • Respiratory irritant affecting lungs and airways
  • Low concentrations can anesthetize nose at 4 ppm (false sense of safety)
  • Accumulates in low areas due to density
  • Reddish-brown color provides visual warning
  • Often present after blasting operations
Health Effects by Concentration:
  • 0-25 ppm: Light nose and throat irritation
  • 4 ppm: Anesthetizes nose (false sense of safety)
  • 20-50 ppm: Cough, mild fatigue, nausea, eye/nose/throat irritation
  • 60-150 ppm: Higher irritation, severe coughing
  • 200-700 ppm: Can be lethal even at short exposure
Detection & Monitoring:
  • Electrochemical NO₂ detectors
  • Reddish-brown color visible at high concentrations
  • IDLH: 20 ppm—very low threshold, immediate evacuation required
  • Mandatory testing after blasting operations

Choke Damp
Molecular Weight
44.0 g/mol
Density vs Air
1.53
Normal Level
0.038%
Primary Hazard
Asphyxiation
Sources in Mines:
  • Decomposition of organic materials
  • Worker and animal respiration
  • Combustion processes
  • Oxidation of coal and minerals
Hazards:
  • Suffocating gas causing asphyxiation
  • Sinks to low areas and dead zones due to high density
  • Displaces oxygen creating low-oxygen atmosphere
  • At 3%: causes breathing difficulty
  • At 18%: can be lethal
  • At 25%: kills quickly
  • Combined with low oxygen: lethal at lower levels (9-10%)
Health Effects by Concentration:
  • 3-5%: Increased lung ventilation, difficult breathing
  • 5-7%: Rapid, labored breathing
  • 7-10%: Dizziness, headache, visual/hearing dysfunction
  • 10-15%: Severe symptoms, unconsciousness within minutes
  • 18%+: Can be lethal
  • 25%+: Quickly lethal
Detection & Monitoring:
  • Electrochemical sensors
  • Maximum safe level: 0.5%
  • Continuous monitoring in low areas and dead zones
  • SCBA required if CO₂ elevated above safe limits

Sulfur Dioxide (SO₂)

Heaviest Gas

64.0 g/mol
Density vs Air
2.26
IDLH
100 ppm
Odor

Sources in Mines:
  • Combustion of sulfur-containing fuels
  • Roasting of sulfide ores
  • Mine fires involving sulfur compounds
  • Blasting in sulfur-rich geological areas
Hazards:
  • Toxic irritant gas affecting respiratory tract
  • Pungent odor provides some warning
  • Blocks nerve signals from lungs (paralysis risk)
  • Accumulates heavily in low areas and floors
  • Can be life-threatening at high concentrations
  • Much heavier than air—sinks rapidly
Health Effects by Concentration:
  • 0-50 ppm: Easy to detect by smell
  • 25-50 ppm: Light nose and throat irritation
  • 50 ppm: Very dangerous for prolonged exposure (> 30 min)
  • 100-200 ppm: Chest tightness, acute bronchitis, possible death
  • > 200 ppm: Rapidly lethal
Detection & Monitoring:
  • Electrochemical SO₂ detectors
  • Pungent odor (0-50 ppm)
  • IDLH: 100 ppm—requires immediate evacuation and SCBA
  • Continuous monitoring in areas with sulfide minerals or combustion sources
